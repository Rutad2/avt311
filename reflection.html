REFLECTION

This semester in AVT 311 helped me slow down and be more intentional with my decisions. Each project pushed me in a different way: one built daily discipline, one let me experiment with expressive typography, and one challenged me to create a cohesive multi-page system. Working through them helped me understand not just how I design, but how I want to approach design moving forward.

“EXPERIMENT. FAIL.
REFLECT. REPEAT.”

A lot of this class was just trying things without really knowing what the result would be. Half the time I didn’t feel like I knew what I was doing, but those “this isn’t working at all” moments actually ended up teaching me the most. I started getting used to stopping and looking back at what I made, figuring out why something felt off or why it actually worked. Reflection kind of became part of the routine, not something extra I had to force.

STARTING OUT

At the start of the semester, my whole approach was basically: think of an idea and just make it. I wasn’t sketching, researching, or trying different versions; I would go straight to the final without slowing down. The early exercises really pushed me out of that habit. Working with point, line, and plane, and doing the symmetric/asymmetric studies, made me pay attention to structure and the basics instead of rushing straight into the finished piece.

GROWTH OVER TIME

As the semester went on, my process became way more iterative. The daily posters showed me how important repetition and small variations can be, and the 2001 project made me realize how much a grid and clear hierarchy actually matter when you're working with a ton of text. I started paying more attention to pacing, alignment, and balance, and I learned how type can act as both communication and imagery at the same time.

LESSONS LEARNED

By the time I got to the zine project, I finally understood how to keep things consistent across multiple pages and how sticking to a limited color palette can actually make the concept stronger. I also learned how to use scale and negative space to create a kind of rhythm from spread to spread. Overall, I became more intentional, more organized, and more willing to experiment. The biggest thing I learned, though, was how to explain why I made certain decisions instead of just what I did.
