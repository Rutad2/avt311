WORK

Some work I have done in class

Projects

Project 1 – Daily Poster Series “Rualiious”

14 daily posters built around a consistent cherry motif. Each poster explores a different visual direction while keeping the same format, theme, and conceptual framework.

Reflection

Project 1 was the first time I had to design on a strict daily schedule, and it ended up teaching me more about discipline than anything else. Creating something new every day forced me to let go of overthinking and just make decisions, even when I wasn’t feeling creative. I experimented a lot with colors, typography, illustration styles, and compositions, and started noticing what I naturally gravitated toward.

Some posters came out cleaner and more intentional, while others felt chaotic or unresolved, but the mix helped me figure out which directions were worth pursuing. By the end, I was more comfortable taking risks, trusting my eye, and committing to an idea instead of restarting over and over. I also learned how setting rules for myself (format, theme, and motifs) actually made the creative part easier.

Project 2 – Type as Text & Type as Image (HAL Monologue)

A nine-sheet typographic composition using HAL’s shutdown monologue. One side uses type as image; the other focuses on type as text, arranged across a larger grid.

Reflection

For this project, I liked the direction I chose and how the final design looked on screen. The monologue gave me a clear emotion to work from, so it was easier to push the typography instead of treating it like a normal layout.

The text side helped me understand how a bigger grid works. I adjusted column widths, leading, and spacing until the monologue felt readable but still had movement. Printing the nine sheets taught me how important it is to think about the physical version early—some details fell in the gaps between pages and didn’t line up the way I expected.

Project 3 – WAVE Zine

A 12-page zine exploring different meanings of the word “wave” through limited color, type systems, and pacing across multiple spreads.

Reflection

Project 3 was the hardest to start. Even after choosing “wave,” I wasn’t sure how to turn the idea into a full set of pages. Working with only a few colors and typefaces made the decisions feel more strict, so I had to rely on structure instead of decoration.

As I built the spreads, I started using everything from earlier in the semester: grids to hold the system together, hierarchy to pace the content, and the texture exercises to control contrast and density. Some pages came out exactly how I wanted and others were harder to resolve, but this project helped me see how all the small exercises connect when you’re working on a larger system.

Exercises

Exercise 1 – Point, Line, Plane

5"×7" compositions translating photographs into abstract arrangements of points, lines, and planes.

Reflection

This was one of the first exercises we did, and it ended up being way more fun than I expected. Translating simple photos into pure points, lines, and planes forced me to stop thinking about “objects” and focus on structure instead. I had to pay attention to angles, spacing, and how shapes interact without relying on color or detail.

It was a good reminder that composition comes first, not decoration. This stuck with me throughout the semester and made me more aware of underlying shapes and grids in later projects, especially typography and the multi-page work.

Exercise 2 – Rhythm & Balance

Symmetric and asymmetric compositions built only from letters of one typeface, focusing on scale, repetition, and spacing.

Reflection

This exercise pushed my creativity more than I expected. At first I didn’t feel like my ideas were very interesting, especially with such limited material. But working through the pairs forced me to pay attention to stroke weight, spacing, and how the same pieces feel completely different depending on arrangement.

Even though I wasn’t in love with every composition, it helped me loosen up and think beyond the literal meaning of letterforms. The practice with movement and structure became really useful later in the poster projects and the zine.

Exercise 7 – Photographed Letters

A series of photographed letters where traditional counters become figure and ground, exploring shape, texture, and legibility.

Exercise 4 – Grid & Cut Paper

Four compositions built from cut paper and type on a strict grid system.

Reflection

Exercise 4 was one of the hardest assignments for me. Working with a strict grid and cut paper felt awkward, and I wasn’t very happy with how my pieces looked at the time. But it forced me to pay close attention to alignment, spacing, and how each element interacted with the grid.

Later in the semester I realized how much it shaped the way I worked. It made me more careful with layout and helped me see how structure can support a design instead of limiting it.

Exercise 5 – Hierarchy

8.5"×11" layouts for a lecture series poster, created using only typography and grid-based hierarchy.

Reflection

Exercise 5 was the first time the grid started working for me instead of against me. I tried different ways of organizing the information and saw how small changes in size or placement affected how people read the page.

I liked how these turned out because they feel much cleaner than my earlier grid work. This exercise made me more confident with type and hierarchy, and I used a lot of what I learned here in the 2001 project and the zine.

Exercise 11 – Text Texture

Six 10"×10" typographic compositions exploring texture using alignment, leading, density, and blocks of text.

Reflection

For this exercise, I focused on how blocks of text create weight and rhythm on a page. Working with justified and flush-left text made me pay closer attention to spacing and texture while keeping everything readable.

It helped me see text as visual material, not just something to read, and that mindset carried into how I approached later typography projects and spreads.
