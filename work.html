Projects

Project 1 – Daily Poster Series “Rualiiious”

14 daily poster designs built around a consistent cherry motif. Each poster explores a different visual direction while maintaining the same theme, format, and conceptual framework.

Reflection

Project 1 was the first time I had to design on a strict daily schedule, and it ended up teaching me more about discipline than anything else. Creating something new every day forced me to let go of overthinking and just make decisions, even when I wasn’t feeling creative. I experimented a lot, different colors, typography, illustration styles, and compositions, and I started noticing what I naturally gravitated toward.

Some posters came out cleaner and more intentional, while others felt chaotic or unresolved, but the mix helped me figure out what directions were worth pursuing. By the end of the project, I was more comfortable taking risks, trusting my eye, and committing to an idea instead of restarting over and over. I also learned how helpful it is to set rules for myself (like format, theme, or visual motifs) because those constraints actually made the creative part easier.

Project 2 – Type as Text & Type as Image

A nine-sheet typographic composition using HAL’s shutdown monologue. I combined type as text and type as image to express tension, emotion, breakdown, and hierarchy across multiple sheets.

Reflection

For this project, I liked the direction I chose and how the final design looked on screen. Working with the HAL shutdown monologue gave me a clear emotion to work from, so it was easier to push the type visually instead of treating it like a normal layout. The image-based sheets were fun to build because I could distort the letters and use scale and color to show the tension in the scene.

The text side of the project helped me understand how a larger grid works. I tried different column widths and spacing until the monologue felt readable but still had some movement across the page. When I finally printed the nine sheets, I realized I should have planned for the gaps between the pages. That taught me how important it is to think about the physical version early in the process.

Project 3 – WAVE Zine

A 12-page zine exploring different meanings of the word “wave” through layout, limited color, and typographic systems. Sequencing and pacing were key to how the narrative progressed.

Reflection

Project 3 was the hardest project for me to get started. Even after choosing “wave,” I wasn’t sure how to turn the idea into a full set of pages. Working with only a few colors and typefaces made the decisions feel more strict, so I had to rely on structure instead of decoration.

As I built the spreads, I noticed how much I was using what we learned earlier in the semester. The grid helped hold everything together, hierarchy guided the pacing, and the texture exercises made me think more about contrast and density. Some pages came out exactly how I wanted and others were harder to resolve, but this was the project where I really saw how all the earlier exercises connect when you’re working on a larger system.

Exercises

Exercise 1 – Point, Line, Plane

Reflection

This was one of the first exercises we did, and it ended up being way more fun than I expected. Translating simple photos into pure points, lines, and planes forced me to stop thinking about “objects” and focus on structure instead. I had to pay attention to angles, spacing, and how shapes interact without relying on color or detail.

It was a good reminder that composition comes first, not decoration. This exercise also stuck with me throughout the semester, because it made me more aware of underlying shapes and grids in every project after this, especially once we got into typography and multi-page layouts.

Exercise 2 – Rhythm & Balance

Reflection

This exercise pushed my creativity more than I expected. At first I didn’t feel like my ideas were very interesting, especially since we were only allowed to use letters from one typeface. But working through the symmetric and asymmetric pairs forced me to pay attention to small decisions—the weight of a stroke, spacing, and how repetition creates rhythm.

Even though I wasn’t in love with every composition I made, the assignment helped me loosen up and start thinking beyond the literal meaning of letterforms. It taught me how much movement and structure you can create just by adjusting scale, repetition, and alignment. That ended up being really useful later in the semester, especially in the poster project and the zine.

Exercise 4 – Grids & Cut Paper

Reflection

Exercise 4 was one of the hardest assignments for me. Working with a strict grid and cut paper felt awkward, and I wasn’t very happy with how my pieces looked in the end. Even so, it taught me something important. I had to pay close attention to alignment, spacing, and how each element interacted with the grid.

Later in the semester, I realized how much this exercise shaped the way I worked. It made me more careful with layout and helped me understand how structure can support a design instead of limiting it.

Exercise 5 – Hierarchy

Reflection

Exercise 5 was the first time I felt like the grid actually started working for me instead of against me. I spent most of the time trying different ways to organize the information and seeing how small changes in size or placement affected the flow of the page.

I liked how these turned out because they feel much cleaner than the earlier grids. This exercise made me pay attention to alignment, spacing, and how people read a page. I used what I learned here in the later projects, especially the 2001 project, where hierarchy and pacing mattered a lot.

Exercise 11 – Type Texture

Reflection

For this exercise, I focused on how blocks of text create weight and rhythm on a page. Working with justified and flush-left text made me pay closer attention to spacing, texture, and how different alignments change the overall feel of a composition.

I had to adjust scale, leading, and density so the layouts stayed readable while still forming a strong visual structure. This assignment helped me understand text as a visual material, not just something to read, which carried into how I approached later typography work.
